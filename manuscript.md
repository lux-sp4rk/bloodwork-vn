# Digital Death Dolls (Manuscript)

Rachel clicked **"Place Order"** on the NinjaMaster 9000 and its companion opponent, Viking Warrior. Tommy would finally have the AI battle toys he’d been begging for. She was about to close the website when the confirmation email dinged in her inbox. At that exact moment, something struck her bedroom window, glass exploding inward.

Rachel screamed as the crow's broken body tumbled onto her laptop, its wings splayed awkwardly, blood smearing across the glowing product photo of the toys.

For a moment, the glowing red eyes of the NinjaMaster seemed to reflect in the bird's unseeing gaze.

Later that morning, after having stiffened her nerves with a double serving of her favorite cocktail: vodka with a lot of ice, a little bit of lemonade, and a side of prozac, Rachel carefully carried the crow's lifeless body into the backyard. The sun was already high, casting long shadows across the dew-kissed grass. She found a small spade in the shed and began to dig a shallow grave beneath the old oak tree. The earth was soft and damp, and the rhythmic scrape of the spade against the soil was the only sound breaking the silence.

"Mommy, what are you doing?"

Rachel startled, dropping the spade. Tommy stood at the back door, his eyes wide with curiosity. He was still in his pajamas, a dinosaur print that suddenly seemed too cheerful for the grim task at hand.

"Nothing, sweetie," Rachel said, forcing a smile. She quickly tried to cover the crow with a pile of leaves.

"Just... planting a new flower."

Tommy tilted his head. "But there's no flower." He pointed a small finger at the barely concealed bird. "And what's that?"

Rachel's heart pounded. "It's just a... a little bird that wasn't feeling well. We're helping it go to sleep." She knelt, pulling him into a hug, trying to shield the sight from him.

"It's okay, honey. Everything's okay."

## Act 1

Later, while researching bird behaviour on the couch, this time mixing her red bull with vodka, Rachel felt drowsy. The caffeine wasn’t doing anything. She knew she’d overdone it today, and that she would go on another binge, and would pay for it later with a horrible hangover, but the image of the earlier crow shattering the window was still fresh in her mind, a vivid and disturbing replay.

She usually slept in the living room because she hated her bed now—it felt too big, too empty—so this was where she ended most nights. Reading while the TV was on, loving the feeling of the alcohol mixed with the pills her doctor prescribed for her anxiety. But, she told herself, she wasn’t addicted or anything. She had only just started. It would be easy to quit if it really became a problem. It was just for now while she got her bearings. She wasn’t going to let it get so bad that it would hurt her son. Like it did that time at the university.

The fluorescent lights of the paleontology lab buzzed overhead, casting a sterile glow on the scattered bones and fossils. Rachel took another swig from the water bottle she’d filled with vodka that morning. It wasn’t water, but it kept her going. She was hunched over a triceratops skull, a fossil that had been in the lab for decades. She'd always had a special fascination with it. Now, she just felt a kinship with the ancient bones. She felt as if everything was dead.
“Rachel, you okay?” Her lab partner, David, an older member of the faculty, with gray hair and a long gray bear, closer to retirement than he felt comfortable admitting, said with his voice low. “You seem a little… out of it today.”
“Just tired,” she mumbled, taking another swig of vodka. The liquid did not go unnoticed by David. He could smell the alcohol oozing out pores. He sighed, but his expression was soft. He’d seen this before. For all her brilliance, and her quick rise at the university, anyone could tell she wasn’t prepared for what happened to her.
Such a loss, so early in her career…something like this could end a career early. It wasn’t fair for a young and popular teacher to fail like this, but then again, he thought, what in life ever was? All David could do is cover for Rachel as well as he could, like everyone else in the department, even the students, were doing.
“Alright,” he said, giving her a knowing look. “Just… try to keep it together, okay? We’ve got the dean coming by later.”
Panic flared in Rachel’s chest. She hadn’t even thought about that. Just then, the lab door swung open, and the dean walked in, a stern expression on his face.
“Good afternoon,” he said, his voice echoing through the lab. “I’m here to check on the progress of the Triceratops project.”
Rachel froze, the water bottle half-full with vodka clutched tightly in her hand. Her heart pounded in her chest. She was going to get fired. Just as the dean approached, a student suddenly stepped forward.
“Professor Rachel has been doing a fantastic job, sir,” the student said, a nervous edge in their voice. “She’s been working late nights, and she’s really made a lot of progress.”
Other students chimed in, their voices overlapping, all praising Rachel’s dedication. David joined in, his voice steady and confident. The dean listened, his expression softening slightly. In the end, he nodded and moved on.
Later, David caught up with her. “You’re a mess, Rachel,” he said gently. “We all covered for you back there, but you can’t keep doing this.”
“I know,” she said, tears welling up in her eyes. “I’m trying.”
The dean had noticed, however, and he didn't see it that way. He called her into his office the next day, put her on leave, and told her that she needed to seek help. He said that she could come back when she was better, but the look in his eyes said the truth: the dismissal was final.
As she drifted off, she couldn’t help but wonder: even if it had just been one of those weird lucid dreams she’d been having, was the crow a messenger, like in the novel? Had it come to deliver a message she couldn’t, or wouldn’t, hear? A chilling thought crept in: perhaps the message was simply death.
She woke up in the middle of the night with a headache. She went to the kitchen, and made herself a vodka cranberry. She couldn’t take any aspirin, for she had been drinking, she knew for sure that mixing those was bad for one’s health, so she made herself a double.
On her way to her room, she paused at Tommy’s doorway. He looked so small and fragile under the blankets.
Rachel lingered for a moment, her chest tightening. She missed her late husband, Captain Branson…Ethan, every day. He had been shorter than her, not by much - she thought of herself as on the short side of tall, but he had carried himself with a commanding presence that filled every room.
She still loved to say “Captain Branson”, letting it linger on her lips like it was a kiss.
He hadn’t died in battle, like so many assumed – an assumption she quietly encouraged – but in a drunken accident at last year’s Thanksgiving dinner. On a dare, he’d climbed out a second-story window, boasting about his mountain goat reflexes. One wrong step, and he was gone. Rachel could still hear his voice sometimes, teasing her: “I must’ve been a mountain goat in a past life!” The memory brought a bitter smile to her lips.
Tommy bore a striking resemblance to his father. Rachel imagined Ethan looking just like that at the same age, a vivid memory flashing of them playing video games only three months prior. For his birthday, Ethan had splurged on the very game he'd always wanted. He had come from a poor background, self-deprecatingly calling himself "proud trailer trash" with a mix of irony and underlying bitterness—a part of himself he usually kept hidden. That drive, that need to escape his circumstances, had fueled his decision to join the army. He'd risen through the ranks with impressive speed, eventually achieving the rank of captain. It was a path he saw as a way out, a way to escape his reality, much like Rachel was trying to do right now.
She told herself she wasn't in trouble yet. Rachel clung to the belief that she could stop her destructive behaviors whenever she chose. She knew she was still in control, that she had researched the depths of her problems and knew where the point of no return would be, and she was far from that. Rachel possessed a sharp, analytical mind; something she was proud of, even now. She was certain she could pull herself back from the edge. She’d done it plenty of times before.
No one knew about her past struggle with bulimia in college, a battle she still sometimes fought in secret. Whenever Ethan returned from his military deployments, the change in her eating habits would resurface, triggered by the fear of his judgment. Shame and desperation made for strange bedfellows. She was now facing a similar problem with her reliance on drugs and alcohol. Isn't alcohol just another drug? she thought. Does that make me an addict? Her current situation felt like another desperate solution, a way to cope with the pain.
Rachel paused, the thought catching her off guard. Addict. It was a label she'd never applied to herself before. She said it outloud: “Addict”, the word hung in the air, heavy and unsettling, leaving an acrid taste that clung to her teeth on the tap of the last “t”.
The memory of Tommy's first day of school was a fresh wound. She’d held his hand, trying to project a confidence she didn't feel, as they walked up to the brightly colored gates. Other parents chatted and laughed, their children skipping ahead, eager to start their day. Tommy, however, clung to her leg, his eyes wide with apprehension.
“It’s going to be okay, sweetie,” Rachel whispered, kneeling down to his level. “You’re going to have so much fun. You’ll make lots of friends.”
He just buried his face in her coat, his little body trembling. When the gates finally opened, and the other children rushed in, Tommy froze. Rachel gently nudged him forward, but he resisted, his grip tightening on her hand.
“Mommy, don’t go,” he whimpered, his voice thick with tears.
“I have to, honey,” Rachel said, her own voice cracking. “But I’ll be back to get you later. I promise.”
As the gates began to close, Rachel forced herself to step back. Tommy’s small face crumpled, and he burst into tears. In a panic, he turned and began to climb the chain-link fence surrounding the schoolyard. His small hands gripped the metal, his sneakers scrabbling for purchase.
“Tommy, no!” Rachel cried out, her voice laced with terror. She saw not her son, but Ethan, falling. The image was so vivid, so real, that she stumbled backward, clutching at her chest. The chain-link fence, though not very high, seemed to loom stories above the ground, every foot reminding Rachel of the height Ethan had fallen.
"Tommy!" she cried again, reaching to try and pull him down. But then she stopped. She couldn't move. She felt paralyzed, rooted to the spot, by a memory so strong it threatened to overwhelm her.
The other parents stared at Tommy, and then at her, and then a teacher approached, trying to coax Tommy down. But all Rachel could see was Ethan, and all she could hear were Tommy's desperate cries. The school faded away. She felt the world tilt, and her knees weakened.
She pulled him out of school that day, and he never returned. The terror in Tommy's eyes, the echo of his cries, vividly reminded her of the day Ethan died. That very week, she sought therapy for her anxiety and had her first experience with Xanax. The following week, she began drinking heavily for the first time in her life, finding that the combination of alcohol and anxiety pills helped her cope, masking the near-constant need to cry. Now, just a month later, she had lost her job, likely ending her career.
A noise woke her. Faint at first, like a mechanical hum, but growing louder. She sat up groggily, her dinosaur mug, a drunken triceratops saying “Go extinct on this drink!!”, half-full of cranberry vodka tipping over onto the carpet.
Then a metallic voice pierced the quiet: "By the blood of the dragon, target confirmed!"
Tommy’s scream followed, sharp and panicked.
“Mom! Mom! Help! Help!”
Rachel bolted up the stairs, her heart pounding in her chest. Every second stretched unbearably as she reached his room. Tommy’s howling grew louder.
She froze in the doorway, horrified. The ninja was jabbing its plastic sword into Tommy’s chest, its servos whining with each precise thrust. Tommy squirmed, his face wet with tears, clawing at the toy with tiny hands that couldn’t make it stop.
Rachel lunged, grabbing the ninja with both hands at the waist. Its limbs twisted unnaturally, gears grinding as it fought to break free. It was stronger than she expected, its sharp movements almost jerking it from her grasp, hurting her wrist.
With a cry, she stumbled to the open window and hurled it outside. The toy disappeared into the darkness. She heard it break when it hit the ground. Closing the window, she locked it, the click of the latch feeling like another door closing on her, trapping her inside.
Act 2
Rachel sat slumped against the bathroom wall, the weight of the day pressing down on her chest, and red vile in the toilet, so much for the cranberry vodka from earlier. The front door was locked and the windows barricaded. Tommy was finally asleep, thanks to a half-dose of sedatives crushed into his milk. She hated herself for doing it—it was reckless, dangerous even—but the boy had been inconsolable after the attack.
Her hands trembled as she rubbed her temples. The Ninja’s attack had been impossible, irrational. It wasn’t just some malfunction—this was something else. She had scoured the internet on her phone, desperate for an explanation, but found nothing. No recalls, no complaints. Just glowing reviews about how fun the toys were.
If Tommy hadn’t cried himself to sleep, chest all scratched up like he’d been attacked by a rat, she wouldn’t have believed it happened.
A few hours later, her stomach growled, breaking the silence. She hadn’t eaten since breakfast. And she’d thrown that up anyway. Her stomach just didn’t seem like it could hold anything down lately. The hallway felt unnervingly quiet as she made her way to the kitchen. Every creak of the floorboards set her nerves on edge. She grabbed a celery stalk and a knife, planning to make peanut butter snacks for herself right now, and leave some for Tommy when he woke up.
Standing in the kitchen, Rachel's mind wandered to the fossils she'd spent years studying. Like the triceratops skull in her lab, these toys were essentially lifeless objects - plastic and circuits instead of calcium and collagen. But something felt wrong about them, fundamentally wrong in a way that her scientific mind struggled to categorize. She'd read articles about artificial intelligence - mostly skimming them during lunch breaks between examining specimens - but this seemed different. AI was supposed to be predictable, programmed. These toys moved with a predatory purpose that reminded her of the ancient carnivore fossils she'd excavated, their teeth marks still visible on prey bones millions of years later.
As a paleontologist, she understood better than most how life evolved through a series of unlikely events, how consciousness itself was perhaps the strangest accident in Earth's history. Could whatever was happening with these toys be something similar - some kind of emergent behavior? No, that was ridiculous. She took another sip of vodka, letting it burn away the thought. More likely, she was experiencing some interaction between her anxiety medication and alcohol, creating vivid hallucinations, like the crow crashing through the window that morning. The toys were probably just toys, and Tommy's scratches could have come from anywhere. Maybe it was some sort of allergic reaction and he’d been scratching himself, perhaps a little too hard. But even as she tried to rationalize it, a deeper instinct - the same one that had helped her identify predator marks on million-year-old bones - told her something was terribly wrong.
She reached for the peanut butter. When she turned back to the counter, the knife was gone.
Her heart stopped. She froze, scanning the room. Then she felt it—a sudden, searing pain in her hand. She screamed, dropping the celery. The Ninja was clinging to her arm, its tiny limbs locked around her wrist, the knife buried deep in her palm.
Blood streamed down her fingers as she swung her arm wildly, slamming the toy into the counter. It clattered to the floor, still gripping the blade. Then, the ninja lunged at her again, its eyes glowing a menacing red. Rachel grabbed a frying pan from the sink and swung hard, catching the toy mid-air. The toy shattered, its pieces scattered like shrapnel across the floor, a macabre trail of broken limbs and leaking circuitry.
Panting, Rachel clutched her hand, the knife wound throbbing. Her blood dripped onto the linoleum, pooling at her feet. She turned toward the stairs, desperate to check on Tommy.
The moment her foot hit the bottom step, she felt a sharp, stabbing pain. A nail—long and rusty, one that hadn’t been there before—was sticking out of the wood, piercing straight through her bare foot.
She cried out, collapsing onto the stairs, her head slamming against the banister.
Shortly after, Rachel jolted awake, pain searing through her body. For a moment, she thought she'd died, or that it was all a nightmare. But the throbbing in her hand and foot, and the blood staining the stairs, confirmed her grim reality.
Through the haze of pain, Rachel dragged herself upright, blood smearing the floor behind her. She forced herself up the stairs, each step agony. She had to get to Tommy. The hallway upstairs was eerily silent except for a faint mechanical whirring, like tiny gears turning.
She pushed open the bedroom door. Tommy was still asleep, his small body curled into a ball under the blanket.
She limped over, sinking onto the bed beside him. She brushed his hair from his face, her hand leaving a faint smear of blood on his cheek. He didn’t stir. The sedatives had done their job too well. Guilt clawed at her, but she shoved it down.
Rachel fell into her usual deep sleep beside Tommy, her eyelids growing heavy, while inside the closet, the Viking's unblinking eyes flickered with a disturbing light
Act 3
Rachel awoke to a strange sound—metal scraping against wood. For a moment, she thought she was dreaming, but the sound grew louder. She sat up, her hand throbbing and her foot aching. The bedroom door was ajar. She didn’t remember leaving it open.
Then she heard it—another sound, this one from the hallway. A faint, metallic clicking. Dragging.
Rachel’s stomach twisted into a knot. She eased off the bed, biting her lip to stifle a cry as she put weight on her injured foot. The makeshift bandage was soaked crimson. Snatching a lamp from the nightstand, her only weapon, she crept to the doorway. The scene in the hallway made her blood run cold. The Ninja, strangely rebuilt and now a horrific jumble of parts, faced the Viking's damaged form. The Ninja, a mangled horror of wires and plastic, wielded the kitchen knife with disturbing precision. The Viking, having discarded its plastic sword, now brandished a large pair of scissors.
The fight was a grotesque ballet of clashing metal and ripping plastic. The Ninja lunged, the kitchen knife a glinting blur, aiming for the Viking's remaining eye. The Viking parried with a clang, the scissors snapping open and close just inches from the Ninja's face. It retaliated with a swift jab, scoring a deep gash in the Ninja's arm, wires spilling out like entrails. The Ninja hissed, a sound of tortured servos and grinding gears, and countered with a wild flurry of blows. The Viking staggered, its metallic joints protesting, but held its ground
The fight raged on, a whirlwind of desperate strikes and near misses. The Ninja’s knife found purchase, slicing through the Viking's plastic armor, sparks flying as it hit wires underneath. The Viking, seemingly cornered, suddenly spun, and with a desperate lunge, grabbed the Ninja's arm in its scissors and with a sickening crunch the arm was severed. The Ninja shrieked, a distorted electronic wail, but pressed its attack with its remaining arm swinging wildly. Using this distraction the Viking brought the scissors high and with a swift, final motion, severed what was left of the Ninja’s headless torso. The Ninja crashed to the ground in pieces as oil or some sort of synthetic lubricant sprayed the wall.
The Viking turned, its glowing eyes locking onto Rachel. For a moment, it stood still, as if processing her presence. Then a faint beep broke the silence, and Rachel’s blood ran cold. She recognized the sound from the Ninja—its targeting mode.
Rachel stumbled backward, slamming the door shut just as the Viking charged. The toy collided with the wood, producing a resounding thud. Under normal circumstances, fighting this Viking wouldn't have been a problem, but with her injured hand and foot throbbing and unbandaged, Rachel knew she wouldn't be able to fight him for long. They needed to run. Limping to the bed, she carefully scooped Tommy into her arms, cradling him protectively against her chest.
“Tommy!” she yelled, shaking him brusquely. He didn’t stir. “Tommy, wake up!”
Ignoring the searing pain in her injured foot, she scooped Tommy into her arms and fled towards the living room. Each step sent shockwaves of agony through her body, but she pressed on. Behind her, the relentless clicking of the Viking's mechanical joints echoed through the hallway, a chilling reminder of its pursuit.
Reaching the living room, she laid Tommy on the couch and turned to face the toy. It hesitated, its glowing eyes flickering between her and the boy. Rachel grabbed a chair and swung it with all her strength, smashing it against the Viking. The toy went sailing through the air, clattering against the far wall.
The pain in her foot hit her like a hammer, and she fell to the floor.
“Come on, you little shit!” Rachel shouted, tears streaming down her face. "Just do it! Just do it!"
She sobbed on the cold floor, time stretching into an eternity of fear and pain. Gradually, the realization dawned that she was still breathing, still alive. The silence that enveloped her was deafening. Where was the Viking?
Trembling, she forced herself to move, her fingers closing around the scissors the toy had dropped—a small, sharp lifeline in this nightmare.
With agonizing slowness, Rachel dragged herself to her feet, every movement sending fresh waves of pain through her battered body. Her eyes darted around the room, searching for any sign of the mechanical menace. Then she saw it. The Viking was trapped, its lower half mangled beyond recognition, inside the fish tank she'd bought months ago in a fit of optimism. The toy's remaining arm flailed uselessly against the smooth glass walls, its eyes still glowing with malevolent purpose, even as it lay defeated and immobile.
Desperation fueled Rachel's thoughts as pain and an overwhelming craving for alcohol clouded her mind. With a sudden flash of inspiration, she limped to the bar, her fingers closing around the neck of a vodka bottle. She took a long, burning swig, the liquid fire momentarily dulling her agony.
Steeling herself, she hobbled back to the trapped Viking, dousing the mechanical menace with the remaining alcohol. Her hands trembling, she fumbled for the lighter on the nearby table, its small flame dancing in the darkness. Grabbing a decorative pillow, she set it ablaze, the fabric quickly engulfed in hungry flames. With a grimace, she tossed the makeshift torch into the fish tank.
The vodka ignited instantly, creating a miniature inferno. Rachel watched, transfixed, as the Viking's plastic body began to warp and melt, its malevolent eyes flickering one last time before being consumed by the flames.
Dawn crept through the windows, Rachel sat on the couch, trembling as the flames in the fish tank dimmed. The Viking was no more. The apartment reeked of burnt plastic and vodka, the air heavy with smoke. Every part of her body screamed in pain—her hand throbbed from the knife wound, her foot ached with every pulse of her heartbeat, and her head pounded from sheer exhaustion.
She limped to Tommy, still sleeping soundly on the couch. His tiny chest rose and fell steadily. She touched his forehead, brushing his hair aside. At least he was okay. “You’re okay,” she whispered, more to herself than to him.
Rachel forced herself into the bathroom to clean her wounds. She winced as she disinfected the gash on her hand, gritting her teeth to keep from crying out. She poured more vodka over her injured foot, the sting bringing tears to her eyes. Wrapping her foot in a clean towel, she secured it tightly with duct tape. It wasn’t perfect, but it would have to do.
Her nerves frayed, she stumbled to the balcony, desperate for a cigarette. The cool early morning air was a welcome relief from the suffocating smell of smoke and melted plastic inside. Leaning against the railing, she took a long drag, the nicotine hitting her system like a lifeline. For a moment, she felt the weight of the night ease, just slightly.
A faint clicking sound jolted her from her thoughts. Her blood ran cold.
The Ninja stood in the doorway, its decapitated head dangling loosely by a few exposed wires, its movements jerky and unnatural. One of its arms was missing, and its legs dragged slightly as it walked, but its glowing eyes burned with relentless fury.
It staggered forward, dragging its broken body with mechanical resolve. In its remaining hand, it clutched a shard of glass—jagged and glinting in the dim light.
The Ninja lunged. Rachel dodged, narrowly avoiding the shard of glass as it slashed through the air. Her injured foot slipped on the slick balcony tiles, and she gripped the railing for balance. The Ninja staggered but quickly turned back toward her, relentless.
She swung wildly, trying to knock the toy away, but her strength was fading. The Ninja climbed onto the balcony railing, its glowing eyes fixed on her. With a guttural scream, Rachel lunged at it, grabbing its tiny body. They struggled, her bloodied hands slipping on its slick plastic. The Ninja stabbed at her arm, the glass shard cutting deep, but she refused to let go.
The struggle tipped them both over the edge.
Aftermath
Tommy woke to the sound of sirens. He sat up groggily, rubbing his eyes. The apartment was a disaster—broken furniture, bloodstains on the floor, and shattered glass everywhere. He wandered to the balcony, where the cool night air rushed in through the open door.
Below, police cars and an ambulance crowded the street. Paramedics worked frantically around a figure lying motionless on the pavement. Beside her, the shattered remains of the Viking were scattered across the concrete.
